## Final Learnings

- Both valproate and carbamazepine are guideline‑consistent first‑line options for focal seizures in children ≤ 2 years; selection is guided by drug availability, tolerability, and the need for laboratory monitoring.
- Aromatic AEDs (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin) are acceptable for focal epilepsy but must be avoided in any child with a documented drug‑induced rash or hypersensitivity.
- Before any change of therapy, clinicians must obtain baseline safety labs (ALT/AST < 2 × ULN, platelet count > 150 × 10⁹/L, ammonia within normal limits) and, when feasible, a serum level of the current AED.
- Dose optimisation is performed first: if the current dose is < 20 mg/kg/day for valproate or < 15 mg/kg/day for carbamazepine and seizure control is suboptimal, the dose should be titrated upward in 25‑50 mg increments every 1–2 weeks before considering drug substitution or adjuncts.
- Exaggerated startles, feeding difficulty, or increased tone in children with cerebral palsy are not automatically attributed to drug toxicity; they require confirmation (e.g., seizure diary or video‑EEG) before altering the regimen.
- The decision hierarchy is: (1) confirm seizure‑free status (≥ 6 months) and adherence ≥ 80 %; (2) verify normal safety labs; (3) maintain the current regimen unchanged; (4) if either seizure control or labs are inadequate, proceed to dose optimisation; (5) if optimisation fails, consider switching to valproate (after normal LFTs) or adding levetiracetam as an adjunct.
- Valproate is stocked in >90 % of district pharmacies at ≈ $0.08 per 100 mg; carbamazepine is similarly stocked at ≈ $0.05 per 100 mg; levetiracetam is 2–3 × more expensive and often unavailable, so it is reserved for documented valproate intolerance or failure.
- Phenobarbital is reserved for refractory cases or when both valproate and levetiracetam are unavailable; it may be used in spastic cerebral palsy only with close monitoring for sedation, otherwise it is avoided.
- Initiation of valproate requires baseline liver function tests and ammonia; if ALT/AST > 2 × ULN or ammonia is elevated, valproate should be deferred and an alternative AED chosen.
- When a child is seizure‑free on a stable valproate dose within the therapeutic window (20–30 mg/kg/day) and safety labs are normal, the protocol mandates continuation of the exact current dose without escalation or adjunct addition.
